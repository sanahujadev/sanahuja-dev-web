For years, the market has repeated a dangerous mantra: "If you have a business, you need a website." And thousands of entrepreneurs have obeyed, hiring 5-page sites thinking that was the ticket to modernity.
<br/>
The reality, however, is mathematical: **Having a website does not mean having traffic**.
<br/>
If your website is static and lacks depth, to Google you are a "digital ghost". You are there, but no one sees you unless they type your exact name. And if only those who already know you find you, you don't have a marketing tool; you have an expensive business card.

<Heading as="h3" size="xs" class="my-4"> Organic vs. Paid Traffic</Heading>

Paid traffic (Ads) is like renting an apartment: you pay to be there, and the day you stop paying, you're out on the street. SEO is like buying the house: you build authority brick by brick. It costs more at the beginning but it is yours forever.

<Heading as="h3" size="xs" class="my-4"> The Battle of Local SEO</Heading>
You are not competing against Amazon. You are competing against the business down the street. Google prioritizes geographic relevance. If someone searches for "Plumber", Google doesn't show them the best in New York, it shows them the best in their neighborhood. A generic "Business Card Web" doesn't tell Google **"I am the authority in G√º√≠mar"**. A Digital Ecosystem with specific landings does.

<Callout data-testid="callout-bibliotecario" type="info" title="How Google really thinks">
  **Google doesn't rank 'websites', it ranks ANSWERS.**
  <br/>
  Its mission is to recommend the best expert. If a user searches for **"web design in G√ºimar"** and you only have a generic "Services" page, Google will choose whoever has a specific page about G√ºimar. Without specific content, there is no paradise.
  <br/>
  To understand why your web doesn't rank, you have to understand how Google has evolved.
  <br/>
  10 years ago, SEO was "basic": you repeated the word "shoe" 50 times and Google thought you were relevant in shoes. Today, Google uses Artificial Intelligence and Natural Language Processing. It no longer looks for "words", it looks for Search Intent.
  <br/>
  Imagine Google as the most demanding librarian in the world:
  <br/>
  <List type="ordered" class="my-4 space-y-4">
    <ListItem>The user arrives and asks: "My back hurts when working".</ListItem>
    <ListItem>Your "Business Card Web" has a services page that says: "We do massages".</ListItem>
    <ListItem>Your competition's website has an article titled: "Lower back pain from teleworking? 5 causes and how decontracting massage helps".</ListItem>
  </List>
  <br/>
  Who will the librarian recommend? Whoever has the best answer.
  <br/>
  Google rewards Semantic Authority. If you demonstrate that you know a lot about a topic (with depth, data and structure), Google tags you as an "Expert" and puts you on the podium. If your content is superficial, it sends you to the basement of page 10.
</Callout>
